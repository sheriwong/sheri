EcoDrop is an educational mobile application that intends to build awareness of global environmental issues and promote environmentally friendly actions through lifestyle challenges.

Design Opportunity

The concept we decided to move forward was gamifying environmentalism, which provides an interesting and fun way to motivate people of all backgrounds to participate in environmental activism. It functions by bringing awareness to sustainable living through in-app goals and tasks.

Persona

Our team identified the audience and proceeded to create three personas. One of the personas is Brian Romano. We focused the guiding principles such as simple, intuitive and digestible navigation, easy access and desired functions, and clear communications to users in order to convey the importance of environmentalism and balance fun while being informative.

User Journey

Here is the user journey map for our beginner user. We have mapped out his general interactions with the app along with his satisfaction level and included his thoughts behind his intentions as well.

By looking at his journey, we can see the highs and lows of his experience. His main goal with the app is to learn about feasible methods he can implement to reduce waste and to be more eco-friendly in general and so, he would have the most satisfaction with our modules feature. This feature would provide him with the information and tips he needs in order to start taking initiatives in improving the environment.

However, there is also a downside to this feature which would frustrate our user. In the modules/checkpoints page, our user would not be able to move past the modules without reading and completing the current one. He would have to complete a quiz and that could be very frustrating for him. This could potentially turn users off from reading the modules as they don’t have the option to read ahead or choose the modules they want to actually read.

Iterations of Features

Originally, our application included a Tamagotchi-like character that the user has to maintain through playing educational games daily. Our team wanted the interface to be clean, so I designed the first iteration of the Challenges page. After discussions, we decided to make the contrast greater. Gillian and I reworked the screens.

User Texting + Findings

After the screens were completed, we conducted user testing. The user test included pre-test questions to gauge habits related to the use of lifestyle or game mobile applications. The tasks allowed the user to walk through normal interaction flows within our application. The post-test questions allow us to determine how well the user understood the application, important features, and their level of interest in our application.

Through A/B testing, it was shown that

  • 100% of participants preferred upon-use onboarding for the home screen and game introduction
  • 100% of participants expressed confusion in how the pages were interconnected, for instance how the homepage and community page fits in with the user goals
  • 100% of participants noted a habit of skipping through text and preferred graphics over text-based communication
  • 75% of the participants sought out tangible feedback for motivation
  • 62% of the participants preferred real-life games and games that were more impactful

As a result, we will shift a bigger focus to goal-oriented tasks. We will offer and highlight a variety of tiers for goals that challenges type 1 users to work towards.

Pivoting

Our team did a sprint session to narrow down the target audiencee. Next, we came up with "How might we's" to tackle the audience's goals. From the sprint session, we went from gamifying environmentalism to having real-life applicable challenges.

Our tasks are comprised of two types, tracking tasks and self-reported tasks. I created the showering under 15 minutes and recycling water challenges. After receiving feedback about including more encouragement and tangible achievements, I incorporated more colour and user feedback so that they can see how much time they have left. Further, when they pause the timer, the water is greyed out because the water is not flowing anymore.

If the user finishes his/her shower within the time constraint, we congratulate the user so the user feels a sense of accomplishment. I also included tangible results of how much water the user saved from taking a shorter shower in forms of 4L milk jugs for equivalent comparisons and easier comprehension.

The self-reporting tasks give users tips and tricks on how to be eco-friendly but rely on the integrity of the users to report doing the activity. Above is a recycle water challenge. Once the user has completed one way to repurpose water, the user has to hold onto the button until a circle forms to complete the challenge which gives the user the agency for interaction.

Logo Design

For the logo, I wanted it to appear friendly and in accordance with the colour palette. I played with the shades of blue and the thickness of the face. I added gradient underneath the earth to make it appear softer and glowing.

Final Features

Takeaways

Overall, I have learned a lot from this course and project. I am satisfied with how our team pivoted and pushed the application forward based on weekly feedback. Through user testing, weekly iterations and communications, we were able to identify the essential core features of our application which includes the user’s progress, challenge type and length, tasks, achievements and social aspect through challenging friends.
