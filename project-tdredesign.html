The TD app redesign is centred around visual cues - making it more intuitive and creates a better flow for doing tasks on the app.

Design Opportunity

Quick user testing has allowed me to find out that young adults tend to use their mobile banking app at least once a week. These apps were used to check their balance, conduct E-Transfers, and/or pay bills. I was able to find an opportunity for UI and UX improvements for these commonly performed tasks.

Design Problem

The screens in checking balances and sending money to others lack visual indications and are text-based.

Pain point #1: Upon entering the TD app, the app prompts the user to choose a task. After choosing, the user is brought to the login page. After logging in, the app then brings the user to the task that they’ve chosen which disrupts the task flow.

Pain point #2: To send money in the current app, the user can either select a previously added recipient, import from their phone’s contact list or add a recipient. When selecting a recipient, there are no shortcuts to find a specific person. Subsequently, the user has to scroll down the list of recipients to find the newly added contact.

UI Sketches and Wireframes

I started sketching layouts of different the accounts page, so the user can easily select the account they want to view. I also sketched how each individual account. For E-Transfer, I explored different ways where the user can access frequent recipients and approaches to add more visuals.

Based on the sketches, I created mid-fidelity grey box wireframes on Figma. I made components for repeated elements for efficiency.

User Testing + Findings

Due to the time constraint of this project, I tested 8 users who use at least one banking app for at least twice a week to check their balance, conduct e-transfers and pay bills.

The findings were as follow:

  • 75% of the users use external apps to manage their expenses and investments and did not find the buttons pertaining to investments useful
  • 50% of the users pay off their credit card bills once a week, thus they want to see the total for the pending transactions
  • 87.5% of the users find the swiping motion to go to other accounts distracting
  • 100% of the users were overwhelmed by the amount of information on the investment cards
  • 50% of the users said that the type of accounts on the account page is not obvious because of the weight of the font

Implementation of findings:

  • Hide the functions that are not often used in a kebab menu.
  • Combine the dashboard and the accounts page
  • Add colours to the cash amount going in and out of accounts
  • Add a pending total in credit card account page
  • Separate and remove some of the information on the investment account cards
  • Thicken the font-weight to show the account type on the cards

App Interactions

Checking Balances

The functions that are not commonly used are accessible in the kebab menu. Each account can be accessed from the dashboard. Transactions can be viewed with the swipe up motion and minimized with the swipe down motion. Unlike the current TD banking app, the user may switch accounts only if they press the back button. The user is able to see the pending amount on the credit card screen.

Investment Account

The CAD TFSA investment account is accessible on the dashboard. On the CAD TFSA screen, I incorporated a card where the user is able to see the unrealized gains and losses. The user is also able to toggle different time lengths to see how their portfolio is doing overtime. The account information is taken out of the card because previously there was too much information on the card. The holdings are on the bottom card. Same as the transactions on the debit and credit, the user can swipe up to see all their holdings.

E-transfer

The user can e-transfer from the dashboard, or go into their chequing or savings account and press the send money button. The most recent contacts that the user has sent money to are on the top. The user can search for an email or phone number to find a contact. From the send money page, the user can still choose which account to send money from. After the user enters an amount, the user is able to review the information before confirming. In the confirmation screen, the home and send money again buttons are on the bottom to assure that the user has gone through the information from top to bottom.

Adding New E-Transfer Contact

In the send money screen, the user is able to add a contact by pressing the plus button. After filling out the information, a pop-up indicates that the contact is successfully added. The enter amount screen follows after the pop-up fades out. In the new flow, the newly added contact is automatically selected and the e-transfer process continues seamlessly which eliminates the second pain point.

Takeaways

Overall, I learned that I would go beyond the requirements to make my project look aesthetically pleasing. I meticulously calculated the coordinates in order for the animation to run smoothly and align with the image (i.e. the animation of the tower when the player wins). In the future, I would like to develop more complex interactive storylines and improve on my foundation in coding. Sometimes when I wanted to go back to the code, it was difficult to find because I did not add enough comments in the code. Moving forward, I should write more comments than code.
